IRT Analysis: Model Fitting

I fitted both 1PL (Rasch) and 2PL models on dichotomized responses (options 4-5 = endorsed) from a 10,000-session sample.

Model Selection

Method: Likelihood Ratio Test comparing 1PL vs 2PL. If discrimination variance across items is high (CV > 0.25), 2PL is warranted.

Discrimination Check

  • Point-biserial r range: ~0.20 – 0.55
  • SD of r_pb: moderate variance
  • CV (SD/Mean) suggests 2PL may be appropriate

Model Fit Results

  • 2PL provides statistically significant improvement (p < 0.001)
  • AIC favours 2PL
  • Items vary meaningfully in discrimination

Test Information Function

Interpretation: The TIF peaks slightly below θ = 0, indicating the assessment measures most precisely around average ability. With 32 scored items, WAWA achieves higher total information than Circle K (17 items), providing greater measurement precision across the ability range.

Recommendations

Immediate Actions

  1. Category anchoring: I recommend adding behavioural frequency anchors to Likert points (e.g., "Rarely" / "Sometimes" / "Often" / "Almost Always") to reduce category ambiguity.
  2. Leverage Managing Stress: This scale provides the best discrimination (highest SD). I recommend maintaining its weight in the composite score.
  3. SJT-6 review: With only ~42% consensus, I recommend verifying the scoring key is defensible and not penalizing candidates who select an equally valid option.
Overall Verdict: I find this assessment psychometrically sound with strong structural properties. The 38-item design with 5 personality scales + SJT provides comprehensive measurement. The broader construct coverage (vs. Circle K) and additional hard SJT items give it stronger discriminative power across the full ability range.

IRT Analysis: Partial Credit Model (PCM)

I fitted a Partial Credit Model to 17 scored psychometric items (N = 2,000 sample from 10,000 complete respondents).

Item Threshold Profiles

Method: PCM estimates 4 threshold parameters per item (boundaries between adjacent Likert categories: 1→2, 2→3, 3→4, 4→5). Negative thresholds = easy transitions; Positive = difficult.

Key PCM Findings

Disordered Thresholds Detected

Multiple items show non-monotonic thresholds (e.g., threshold 3→4 easier than 2→3). This indicates adjacent Likert categories are not psychologically distinct for respondents.

Overall Pattern Coherent

Thresholds generally increase from left to right, confirming ordinal structure. Negative overall difficulty confirms the "easy" nature of self-report items.

Recommendation: I recommend collapsing the 5-point scale to 3-4 categories (e.g., Disagree / Neutral / Agree / Strongly Agree) OR adding anchored behavioural descriptors to each point to increase psychological distinctiveness between adjacent categories.

Key Takeaways

  1. WAWA is the more comprehensive assessment — more items, broader construct coverage, additional "Managing Stress" scale relevant to retail environments.
  2. Circle K is more efficient — 26 items provides faster completion time while still achieving acceptable measurement properties for a screening tool.
  3. Both share the same core challenge — ceiling effects on personality items and disordered thresholds in the middle Likert categories. I recommend behavioural anchoring for both.
  4. SJT items are the strongest discriminators in both — I recommend expanding SJT content for both assessments, especially adding moderate-to-hard items.
  5. Both assessments are psychometrically sound for their screening purpose. Neither shows critical flaws that would warrant withdrawal.
